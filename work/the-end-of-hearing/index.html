When I moved to London, I was surprised by the levels of noise experienced in everyday life in the city. Inspired by this and my previous research into CETI, I created The End of Hearing — a collection of three science-fiction stories designed to elicit viewers' reactions towards possible futures of hearing.

The first scenario, 'Artificial Silence', is about regulating the sensitivity of hearing through the use of ototoxic antibiotics (one of their side effects is hearing damage). In the future the Otomixer would allow its users to mix ototoxic doses that can turn off one's hearing for a selected duration and in a chosen frequency range.

The second scenario, 'Blasting!', explores the future of sound conditioning — a novel medical intervention that can protect against noise truma and prevent hearing loss. I look at new extreme sports that could be enabled by sound conditioning.

The last scenario titled 'Voices Inside My Head' imagines a future in which human brains can be augmented to sense radio signals and wireless communication. I created the AIR Implant as a prop for discussing this scenario — it is an actual crystal AM radio receiver tuned to 912.5 kHz (BBC Radio 5) in a gelatine capsule.

I presented The End of Hearing in a public show in London in 2011. I used the scenarios and objects to provoke a discussion with my viewers about their attitudes towards hearing and noise pollution.

I also had a survey with 10 questions about the perception of noise, the role of hearing in peoples' lifes, and the role of design in shaping futures. I collected replies from 65 viewers.

And these are the results of my survey in summary:
  • my respondents believed that future can be shaped and that design can help us do this,
  • my respendents valued hearing and thought that we can adapt to future soundscapes,
  • my respendents were undecided about the role that noise played in our lives.

I made The End of Hearing objects myself, which was great fun. The Otomixer and the plastic blister for AIR Implants were made in the old wood and plastics workshops at Central Saint Martins College of Art and Design in Holborn.

I made The End of Hearing objects myself, which was great fun. The Otomixer and the plastic blister for AIR Implants were made in the old wood and plastics workshops at Central Saint Martins College of Art and Design in Holborn.

I prototyped all working electronic components used in The End of Hearing objects myself. I started by building radio receivers and transmitters, and experimenting with sound amplification.

The Otomixer houses a graphic sound equalizer. I built it from scratch using standard electronic components, such as the LM741 integrated circuit for equalization and the LM386 for amplification.

The Otomixer houses a graphic sound equalizer. I built it from scratch using standard electronic components, such as the LM741 integrated circuit for equalization and the LM386 for amplification.

The AIR Implant is a little crystal radio made from a germanium diode, a ceramic capacitor and an inductance coil. They were picked to resonate in 912.5 kHz (BBC Radio 5). I assembled about 60 is those little radios.
