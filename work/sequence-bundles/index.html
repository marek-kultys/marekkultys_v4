We created Sequence Bundles to help bioinformaticians discover and explore sequence motifs in their protein or DNA Multiple Sequence Alignments (MSAs).

The Sequence Bundles method is a cousin of parallel coordinates. The X-axis shows all sequence positions (here 36). The Y-axis lists all possible residues in the sequence (here 20 amino acids + gap). Each sequence is represented with a line. Lines are continuous and semi-transparent, so it is easy to see as they converge in regions of consensus, and diverge in areas of variability.

Sequence Bundles is available online as a visualisation web-app. We developed it at Science Practice to enable quick and easy visualisation of protein data. Images generated with the web-app can be used for publications.

Sequence Bundles have been also implemented in Alvis — an open-source desktop platform for non-aggregative visualisation and explorative analysis of MSAs. We created this in collaboration with the Goldman group at the European Bioinformatics Institute (EMBL-EBI).

We worked on the Sequence Bundles project at Science Practice for almost 2.5 years. The project started with a few sketches, a couple of interviews with experts, and a lot of research into visualisation in bioinformatics. We then went through several cycles of design iterations: from concept sketches, to design mock-ups, to proof-of-concept prototypes. The project concluded in building a potent visual analystics tool for bioinformatics. I outlined our design journey on the Science Practice blog.
