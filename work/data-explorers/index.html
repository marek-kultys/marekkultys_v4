Data scientists work with huge datasets generated in, for example, genomics or social media. They often build software tools to help them visualise and understand those huge sets of data. Yet, many data scientists lack basic design skills to make good visualisation choices.

The workshop starts with a 90-minute talk with an introduction to basic elements of visual design: a dot, a line, a plane, colour, typography, grid. I discuss examples of good and poor use of those elements. This gives workshop participants not only some theoretical basics of visual design, but also good inspiration for the following partcical part of the workshop.

The introduction is followed by a hands-on exercise. Workshop participants are given a small data set with a few features that are difficult to visualise. Using only pencils and paper (no computers allowed!), participants have to think outside of the box to come up with best ways to visualise and interpret the data.

In the last part of the workshop participants (or groups) present their work to others and discuss their design decisions. A public crit allows everyone to see a range of possibilities in which the dataset can be visualised. It also helps to understand how different design choices influence one's perception of data.
